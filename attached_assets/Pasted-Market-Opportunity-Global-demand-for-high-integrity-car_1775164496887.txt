Market Opportunity
Global demand for high integrity carbon removals is increasing as major economies tighten
decarbonization commitments. Remaining within a 1.5°C pathway requires a 43% reduction in global
emissions by 2030. The European Union has adopted a 90% emissions reduction target for 2040 and
plans to accept credits from developing countries for compliance beginning in 2036. Across the Gulf
region more than $100 billion has been committed to national net zero plans, and corporate procurement
is shifting toward verified removal credits as regulators and buyers treat removals as the credible
instrument for balancing residual emissions.
That demand contrasts with a persistent shortage of verified removal supply. Nowhere is the gap more
visible than in Africa, which holds more than 30% of the world’s nature-based removal potential yet
captures only a small fraction of what it could issue each year. Most African issuance to date comes from
lower-impact categories, while the premium agroforestry, restoration, and reforestation removals that
institutional buyers prefer remain underdeveloped. This creates a structural imbalance in which the
regions best positioned to supply high-integrity removals contribute only a small share of verified global
supply.
Africa’s natural carbon sinks represent one of the largest untapped concentrations of removal capacity
worldwide, with roughly 674 million hectares of forest and the Congo Basin absorbing about 1.2 billion
tonnes of CO₂ each year. Independent analyses suggest that a well-governed African carbon market
could generate between 6 and 30 billion dollars annually by 2030 and scale toward 250 billion dollars by
2050 as more land becomes eligible for crediting. Regional initiatives such as the Africa Carbon Markets
Initiative reflect this trajectory by targeting an expansion from 22 million tonnes of annual issuance in
2021 to 300 million tonnes by 2030. Market data shows that institutional buyers consistently pay higher
prices for verified nature-based removals, particularly agroforestry and restoration credits.
Despite demand, structural barriers prevent rural smallholders from participating at scale. The principal
constraint is the absence of systems that produce continuous, auditable, plot level data acceptable to
registries. Without verified plot level evidence, registries will not issue credits and buyers will not transact
with confidence. The cost of monitoring, the complexity of verification standards, and uncertainty around
land tenure hinder meaningful smallholder participation, while historical integrity issues add further
